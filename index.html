REP // 01 — The Thesis

Reparations as infrastructure, not just checks.

The gap reparations is meant to close is structural. It shows up as missing wealth, blocked mobility, thin institutions, and absent infrastructure — the compounded result of policy that was designed, written down, and enforced over generations. A one-time transfer can acknowledge that. It rarely changes it.

Cash distributed into a system still missing its load-bearing structure tends to leak back out the same gaps that created the harm. So the lever this vertical works on is the system itself: land, housing, education, capital access, and mobility — the durable assets that determine whether a community can hold and compound value rather than watch it drain.

Reparative capital is most effective when it builds or redirects infrastructure that keeps working long after the headline fades, governed by the people it's meant to serve. That draws a hard line between the symbolic and the material.

A statement, a fund announcement, or a logo on a report is signaling. A capitalized institution, a recovered land position, a cooperative that owns its own balance sheet — that is a system change. We design for the second kind, and we measure projects by whether the structure actually shifts.

REP // 02 — Frameworks

From community harms to community repair.

Not every project labeled reparative is doing reparative work. We use a simple evaluative discipline — closer to a structural checklist than a mission statement — to separate capital that changes a system from capital that mostly changes a narrative. A project earns the term when it can answer all three of the checkpoints below, in concrete terms.

CHECKPOINT 01

Acknowledgement & record

  • The specific harm is named, not gestured at.
  • It is documented with data and local knowledge.
  • The affected community is identified precisely.
  • The record is public and can be audited.
CHECKPOINT 02

Infrastructure & capital

  • Tangible assets are built, recovered, or redirected.
  • Institutions and orgs are capitalized, not just consulted.
  • Ownership and governance sit with the community.
  • The capital structure is durable, not one-cycle.
CHECKPOINT 03

Future-proofing

  • The policy or rule that enabled the harm is changed.
  • Tracking and accountability outlive the project team.
  • Gains are protected against extraction or reversal.
  • The same harm cannot be quietly recreated.
REP // 03 — Use-cases

How this lab thinks about a project.

Illustrative, not active engagements — sketches of the reasoning we'd bring to a real diagnosis. Each pairs a structural change with the capital it would actually take to make it hold.

Use-case A · Land & Housing

A historically redlined corridor

A corridor where decades of redlining suppressed ownership and starved property of investment. The repair isn't a payment to residents — it's restoring a community's ability to own, hold, and benefit from the land it lives on.

What changes

Community-controlled land ownership, anti-displacement structures, and reinvestment that accrues to residents rather than incoming capital.

What capital is needed

Acquisition and a community land trust, patient debt for rehabilitation, and a reserve that absorbs the early years of carry.

Use-case B · Institutions

Capitalizing Black-led institutions

Schools, clinics, and cooperatives that already serve a community but operate undercapitalized and debt-burdened. The lever is balance-sheet strength, not another program grant.

What changes

Debt relief and durable endowment move institutions from year-to-year survival to long-horizon operation and ownership.

What capital is needed

Debt retirement, an endowment or recoverable-grant pool, and governance support so capital stays accountable to the community.

Use-case C · Workforce & Mobility

Infrastructure for high-risk labor markets

Communities built around low-wage, high-risk work, where the missing infrastructure is the pathway out — training, transit, and access to better-paying, safer sectors.

What changes

Durable workforce and mobility infrastructure that raises earning power and reduces exposure to dangerous, dead-end labor.

What capital is needed

Capital for training institutions, transit and digital-access infrastructure, and wage-bridge financing through the transition.

REP // 04 — Work with us

For institutions building reparative capital.

This vertical is a research and design arm. It does not collect reparations claims, process applications, or disburse payments to individuals. It works with organizations on the structures that move reparative capital.

We work with cities, foundations, mission-driven funds, anchor institutions, and policy organizations that are serious about moving beyond statements — and want the capital and infrastructure design to match the intent.

The work runs in three phases. First, diagnosis: naming the specific harms and structural gaps with data and local knowledge, so the intervention targets the right load. Then design: the capital and infrastructure interventions — the assets, institutions, and financing structures that carry the repair. Then governance: the tracking and accountability frameworks that keep a project honest and on-thesis for as long as the capital is deployed.

Who this is for

  • Cities and public agencies designing local programs.
  • Foundations and mission-driven funds.
  • Anchor institutions — universities, hospitals, HBCUs.
  • Black-led orgs, cooperatives, and CDFIs.
  • Policy and research organizations.

What we do together

  • Diagnose harms and structural gaps with the evidence.
  • Design the capital and infrastructure interventions.
  • Structure ownership and community governance.
  • Build tracking so projects stay accountable over time.
  • Pressure-test the thesis before capital is committed.
