REP // 02 — Frameworks

From community harms to community repair.

Not every project labeled reparative is doing reparative work. We use a simple evaluative discipline — closer to a structural checklist than a mission statement — to separate capital that[...]

CHECKPOINT 01

Acknowledgement & record

  • The specific harm is named, not gestured at
  • It is documented with data and local knowledge
  • The affected community is identified precisely
  • The record is public and can be audited
CHECKPOINT 02

Infrastructure & capital

  • Tangible assets are built, recovered, or redirected
  • Institutions and orgs are capitalized, not consulted
  • Ownership and governance sit with the community
  • The capital structure is durable, not one-cycle
CHECKPOINT 03

Future-proofing

  • The policy or rule that enabled the harm is changed
  • Tracking and accountability outlive the project team
  • Gains are protected against extraction or reversal
  • The same harm cannot be quietly recreated
REP // 03 — Use-cases

How this lab thinks about a project.

Illustrative, not active engagements — sketches of the reasoning we'd bring to a real diagnosis. Each pairs a structural change with the capital it would actually take to make it hold.

Use-case A · Land & Housing

A historically redlined corridor

A corridor where decades of redlining suppressed ownership and starved property of investment. The repair isn't a payment to residents — it's restoring a community's ability to own, hol[...]

What changes

Community-controlled land ownership, anti-displacement structures, and reinvestment that accrues to residents rather than incoming capital.

What capital is needed

Acquisition and a community land trust, patient debt for rehabilitation, and a reserve that absorbs the early years of carry.

Use-case B · Institutions

Capitalizing Black-led institutions

Schools, clinics, and cooperatives that already serve a community but operate undercapitalized and debt-burdened. The lever is balance-sheet strength, not another program grant.

What changes

Debt relief and durable endowment move institutions from year-to-year survival to long-horizon operation and ownership.

What capital is needed

Debt retirement, an endowment or recoverable-grant pool, and governance support so capital stays accountable to the community.

Use-case C · Workforce & Mobility

Infrastructure for high-risk labor markets

Communities built around low-wage, high-risk work, where the missing infrastructure is the pathway out — training, transit, and access to better-paying, safer sectors.

What changes

Durable workforce and mobility infrastructure that raises earning power and reduces exposure to dangerous, dead-end labor.

What capital is needed

Capital for training institutions, transit and digital-access infrastructure, and wage-bridge financing through the transition.
